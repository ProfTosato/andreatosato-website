# Notes: Digital Assets and the Property Question
## Tosato & Odinet, Florida Law Review (forthcoming 2026)

## 1. Research Question
The article addresses the **Property Question**: a tripartite inquiry into (a) whether digital assets are property under American law, (b) how they should be classified within existing property taxonomies, and (c) what rules govern their commercial circulation. The stakes are enormous: billions traded daily, collateral arrangements, stolen crypto recovery, platform bankruptcies (FTX, Celsius, Genesis), good faith purchaser protections, tax treatment.

## 2. Audience
Commercial law scholars, property law scholars, practitioners dealing with digital asset transactions, bankruptcy lawyers, regulators, policymakers. Sits at intersection of property law theory and UCC commercial law practice.

## 3. Method / Approach
Comparative and historical analysis. Contrasts U.S. approach with other common law jurisdictions (UK, Singapore, etc.). Traces evolution from formalistic categories (choses in action / choses in possession) to functional pragmatism under the UCC. Four-part structure:
- Part I: Market survey and framing of Property Question
- Part II: Whether digital assets are property (comparative)
- Part III: Classification within property taxonomies (comparative)
- Part IV: Commercial circulation rules, culminating in 2022 UCC Amendments (Article 12)

## 4. Key Theses (from Introduction, pp. 1-12)

**Thesis 1 (Primary):** American law has pragmatically accepted digital assets as property and focused on facilitating their commercial circulation, while other common law jurisdictions got bogged down in formalistic classification debates.

**Thesis 2 (Secondary):** Over the 20th century, U.S. personal property law progressively abandoned traditional classifications (choses in possession / choses in action) in favor of a functional, pragmatic architecture. The UCC is the primary engine of this transformation. Leading property scholarship (Calabresi-Melamed, progressive property, exclusion/information-cost) focused on land, leaving personal property theoretically understudied.

**Thesis 3 (Normative):** UCC Article 12, through the innovative category of "controllable electronic records" (CERs), provides a functional taxonomy that dispels persistent legal uncertainties, empowers market participants, and establishes the foundation for effective regulatory frameworks.

## 5. Key Quotable Passages (pp. 1-12)
